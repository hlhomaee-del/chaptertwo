Lesson 1: Who is who in health insurance?

Learn the people and the money roles first. This is the base of the whole chapter.

Key conceptsPolicy means the insurance contract itself.Policyholder means the person who owns that contract.Third-party payer means the insurance company or government program that pays covered claims.Deductible means the amount the patient pays first before the plan starts paying covered benefits.
ExplanationA health insurance policy is a contract. The person attached to ownership of that contract is the policyholder. The outside entity that provides coverage and pays covered claims is the third-party payer. Even when insurance exists, the patient may still owe a portion first, and that amount may be the deductible.
ExampleMaria signs up for family coverage through work. Maria is the policyholder because the contract is in her name. Blue Cross is the third-party payer because it pays covered claims. If Maria has not met her deductible, she pays that amount first before the plan starts paying covered charges.

Lesson 2: Types of coverage

Do not blend these terms together. Each coverage type answers a different question.

Key conceptsGroup health insurance is coverage tied to an employer or organization.Individual health insurance is coverage a person or family buys directly.Public health insurance is government coverage for eligible individuals.
ExplanationThese terms answer different questions. Group coverage asks whether the plan comes through a job or organization. Individual coverage asks whether the person buys the plan directly. Public coverage asks whether the program is a government plan such as Medicare, Medicaid, CHIP, or TRICARE.
ExampleA worker insured through her company has group health insurance. A freelancer who buys a marketplace plan has individual health insurance. A child covered by CHIP is using public health insurance.

Lesson 3: Major laws and historical milestones

This part is about matching the law to the purpose. Stop trying to memorize random names without a job attached to each one.

Key conceptsHill-Burton Act funded hospital construction and modernization.Medicaid is Title XIX and covers eligible low-income individuals.COBRA allows eligible workers to continue coverage after job-related benefit loss if they pay the premium.
ExplanationLaws are easier to remember when you attach each one to one job. Hill-Burton is about hospitals. Medicaid is about a public program for eligible low-income people. COBRA is about continuing coverage after benefits would otherwise end.
ExampleIf the test asks which law helped modernize hospitals after the Depression and World War II, think Hill-Burton. If it asks which program is Title XIX for eligible low-income individuals, think Medicaid. If it asks how a worker keeps health coverage after leaving a job, think COBRA.

Lesson 4: Claims, forms, and billing infrastructure

These questions test which form or system fits the service setting.

Key conceptsCMS-1500 is the claim form for professional outpatient and office services.Per diem means reimbursement based on a daily rate.Physician referral is a written PCP order that sends the patient to a specialist.
ExplanationBilling questions often mix forms, payment methods, and care-management tools. Keep them separate. A claim form tells you what document is used to bill. A payment term tells you how money is calculated. A referral tells you how the patient is directed to another provider.
ExampleA physician office billing Medicare for an outpatient visit uses the CMS-1500. If the question asks about payment by day, that is per diem. If the PCP sends the patient to a cardiologist, that is a physician referral.

Lesson 5: Managed care and cost control

Now move into referral rules, utilization controls, and how plans manage cost.

Key conceptsReferral sends a patient from the PCP to a specialist.Preauthorization / precertification means the plan must approve certain services before they happen.Capitation means the provider is paid a fixed amount instead of billing each service separately.
ExplanationManaged care controls cost and access by setting rules before care is delivered. One rule decides whether the patient needs a referral to see a specialist. Another rule decides whether the plan must approve a service in advance. Payment rules may also change, and one example is capitation, which pays a fixed amount rather than paying separately for each service.
ExampleIf an HMO patient wants to see an orthopedist, the PCP may need to issue a referral. If the patient needs a planned surgery, the plan may require preauthorization. If a provider group is paid one set amount per member each month, that arrangement is called capitation.
