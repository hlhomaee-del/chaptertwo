Foundations of Health Insurance

Start here. These terms look similar, but they do different jobs.

Professional explanationHealth insurance basics revolve around who owns the policy, who pays claims, what patients owe first, and how the medical record supports continuity of care. A third-party payer provides coverage, the policyholder owns the contract, and a deductible is the amount the patient pays before the plan starts paying covered benefits.
Simplified versionOne person owns the plan, one payer covers services, and the patient may still owe money before coverage kicks in.
ExampleExample: A patient comes in for a preventive visit. The payer processes the claim, the policyholder is the person on the plan, and the deductible may matter for non-preventive services.

Coverage Types and Big Programs

This is where students confuse group, individual, and public coverage.

Professional explanationGroup health insurance is usually employer-subsidized. Individual health insurance is purchased directly when employer coverage is unavailable. Public health insurance includes programs such as Medicare, Medicaid, CHIP, and TRICARE. The Affordable Care Act focused heavily on reforming private health insurance, and COBRA allows eligible workers to continue employer coverage if they pay the required premiums.
Simplified versionGroup = through work. Individual = bought on your own. Public = government programs.
ExampleExample: Someone loses a job but wants to keep the same plan. COBRA may let that happen, but the person usually has to pay the premiums.

Major Laws, Claims, and Documentation

Do not memorize law names as random trivia. Tie each one to one outcome.

Professional explanationThe Hill-Burton Act provided funds to modernize hospitals. The Taft-Hartley Act indirectly supported labor-management benefit structures and TPAs. ARRA advanced EHR meaningful use measures. CMS-1500 is the professional claim form for outpatient and office services. Problem-oriented records and SOAP notes organize patient documentation clearly.
Simplified versionConnect each law or system to one clear thing it changed: hospitals, labor plans, EHRs, claim forms, or charting.
ExampleExample: If the question says outpatient or office professional claim, think CMS-1500. If it says the patient reports symptoms in their own words, think Subjective.

Managed Care Models and Cost Control

This part is all about referrals, utilization review, capitation, HMO models, PPOs, and POS plans.

Professional explanationManaged care is designed to coordinate services, control costs, and monitor quality. PCPs coordinate care. Referrals, second surgical opinions, precertification, and utilization management are cost-control and review tools. PPOs use discounted networks with more flexibility. HMOs have several delivery models, including staff, group, IPA, and direct contract. POS plans blend flexibility by allowing in-network care or out-of-network self-referral.
Simplified versionHMO = more structure. PPO = more freedom. POS = a mix. Utilization management checks whether care is necessary and appropriate.
ExampleExample: Before elective surgery, a plan may require precertification or a second surgical opinion. If a patient needs a specialist, the PCP may write a referral.
